OpenHarmony has emerged as a viable third ecosys- tem in mobile operating systems, complementing Android and iOS. To support its adoption, significant efforts have been devoted to porting C/C++ libraries from Linux to OpenHarmony. However, this porting process presents unique challenges due to fundamental architectural differences in system libraries, run- time environments, and build systems between the two platforms. These discrepancies manifest as Cross-platform Compatibility (CPC) issues during cross-compilation, which are particularly difficult to resolve for two key reasons. First, conventional cross-compilation toolchains provide only cryptic error messages that offer inadequate diagnostic information for CPC issues. Second, resolving these issues requires deep understanding of cross-platform discrepancies, yet comprehensive documentation or systematic guidelines about such Linux-to-OpenHarmony differences remain largely unavailable.

To assist developers in addressing these challenges, we con- ducted an empirical study on 92 C/C++ libraries successfully ported to OpenHarmony. Through manual step-by-step repro- duction of all CPC issues, our study reveals that discrepancies between OpenHarmony and Linux can be divided into three cat- egories, and CPC issues can manifest through eight dimensions. Furthermore, we identified eight common adaptation strategies for addressing CPC issues. Based on these findings, we present CROSS2OH, an automated technique for porting Linux-based software to OpenHarmony. Our approach combines: (1) an adap- tation knowledge base (derived from RQ1 and RQ2 findings) with (2) static analysis to detect and patch eight types of CPC issues. Tightly integrated with the LYCIUM framework, CROSS2OH automates the entire cross-compilation process. Evaluation using real developer patches shows 0.94 recall and 0.91 precision in resolving CPC issues. Notably, we successfully ported 36 critical libraries (including dependencies for WeChat, Microsoft Excel, and Bilibili, etc.), all passing official OpenHarmony review.
